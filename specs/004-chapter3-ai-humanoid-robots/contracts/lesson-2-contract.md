### Section 1: From Fixed Machines to Flexible Helpers (~400 words)

#### H3 1.1: How Old Robots Worked
- Traditional industrial robots: fixed, caged, repetitive
- Programmed for one task only
- Dangerous for humans to be near
- Example: car assembly line robots (same motion forever)

#### H3 1.2: Why Flexibility Matters Now
- Products change, customer needs change
- Factories need to adapt quickly
- Workforce shortages in many countries
- Economic pressure to do more with less

#### H3 1.3: AI Makes the Difference
- Intelligence enables adaptation
- Robots can now see, decide, adjust
- Learn new tasks without complete reprogramming
- Reference: "moving from automation to autonomy"

#### H3 2.3: Real Factory Examples

**Expert Insight #1** (place after Section 2):
```markdown
:::info Expert Insight: Robots Working Beside Humans
Unlike old factory robots that had to be kept behind safety cages, modern cobots are designed to be teammates. They can sense when a human is nearby and adjust their movements to be safe. In many factories, workers now have robot partners that handle the heavy lifting while humans focus on tasks that need creativity and judgment. It's like having a super-strong, never-tired assistant.
:::
```

### Section 3: Learning and Adapting on the Job (~400 words)

#### H3 3.1: How Robots Learn Tasks
- Machine learning basics (simple explanation)
- Learning from examples and practice

- Reinforcement learning (trial and error)

#### H3 3.2: Switching Between Jobs
- Same robot, different tasks
- Morning: packaging products
- Afternoon: moving boxes
- Reduces need for specialized machines

#### H3 3.3: The Smart Factory of Tomorrow
- Digital twins: virtual copies for testing
- Connected systems working together
- Robots embedded in digital ecosystems
- Reference: "adaptive, flexible, and connected operations"

**Expert Insight #2** (place after Section 3):
```markdown
:::info Expert Insight: Why AI Gives Robots Flexibility
Traditional factory robots needed expert programmers to teach them every single movement. Today's AI-powered robots can learn by watching, practicing, and adjusting — much like how you learn. This means a robot that packed boxes yesterday can learn to inspect products today. Industry reports call this shift "from automation to autonomy" — robots that can think and adapt, not just repeat.
:::
```
